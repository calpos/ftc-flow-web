# Product

## Register

brand

## Users

Two audiences land here, both skeptical and both fast to leave. FTC students (13 to 18) arrive from a Discord link or a teammate's message, usually on a phone, deciding in under twenty seconds whether this is credible enough to show their team captain. Adult mentors and coaches arrive deliberately, evaluating whether a tool built by a high schooler is something they can trust a season's coordination to. Neither is here to browse: the job is "convince me this is real, show me what it actually does, and tell me what happens next."

## Product Purpose

This is the marketing site for FTC Flow, a calm dark coordination app for FIRST Tech Challenge teams (tasks, projects, calendar, polls, team management). The app is pre-launch: TestFlight beta in summer 2026, App Store in fall 2026, a full web portal after that. The site exists to convert visits into early-access mailing list signups and to establish credibility before launch. Success is a student forwarding the link to their team, and a mentor signing up without wondering whether the product is vaporware. The site sells the app; it is not the app. No real auth, no app features.

## Brand Personality

Sharp, confident, plain-spoken. The voice of a competent teammate who built the thing they needed, not a company pitching one: "built by a competitor, not a committee." Where the app is deliberately quiet, the site is allowed to be cinematic; it earns attention with large type, real screenshots, and one blue light source, never with hype. Three words: focused, credible, ambitious. Never corporate, never cutesy, never breathless.

## Anti-references

- **The corporate SaaS landing template**: hero metric, logo wall, three-column icon-heading-text feature cards, fake testimonial carousel. This is a builder's site, not a Series A deck.
- **Fake traction**: no invented adoption numbers, no testimonials, no "trusted by N teams." The app is pre-launch and the honesty IS the positioning.
- **AI-slop gradients**: no gradient text, no rainbow meshes, no purple-to-cyan washes. One blue, used like a light source.
- **Childish or gimmicky**: no mascots, no confetti, no gamified energy. Credibility with serious teams is the entire asset.

## Design Principles

1. **The screenshots are the argument.** Real app UI, framed like a product, does the persuading. Every section's job is to get a real screen in front of the visitor; copy supports the image, not the reverse.
2. **Cinematic restraint.** The site may move and glow where the app stays still, but every effect points at the product: a reveal brings a screenshot into view, a glow backlights a device. Decoration that points at nothing gets cut.
3. **Honest pre-launch.** Roadmap framing over launch theater. "Coming Fall 2026" stated plainly, disabled states labeled honestly, shipping in public as the narrative.
4. **One voice, louder.** Signal Blue remains the interface's single voice, inherited from the app; the web just turns up its wattage. If two blue elements compete in one viewport, one is wrong.
5. **The fifteen-second pitch.** The landing hero must answer "what is it, who is it for, what do I do next" before the first scroll, the same way the app answers "what do I do next" in fifteen seconds.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

WCAG AA contrast throughout (text on every tonal layer checked). `prefers-reduced-motion` disables or reduces all scroll and entrance motion to simple fades or nothing. Visible focus states on all interactive elements; semantic HTML landmarks per page. Fully responsive 360px through 1536px+. Screenshots carry descriptive alt text written in the product voice.
